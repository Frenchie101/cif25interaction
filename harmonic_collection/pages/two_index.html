What Is Colorblindness?

Colorblindness, also known as color vision deficiency, is a condition that affects the way a person perceives colors. Individuals with colorblindness may have difficulty distinguishing certain colors—most commonly reds, greens, and sometimes blues. Instead of seeing the full spectrum of color, people with color vision deficiency may see some colors as muted, similar, or easily confused.

The condition occurs when the cone cells in the retina—responsible for detecting color—are missing, functioning improperly, or responding differently to light. Normally, three types of cones sense red, green, or blue wavelengths. When one type is altered or absent, color perception is affected.

Types of Colorblindness

Red–Green Colorblindness (Most Common)

This type makes it difficult to distinguish reds from greens or may cause these colors to appear dull. It includes:

  • Protanomaly / Protanopia – red cone deficiency
  • Deuteranomaly / Deuteranopia – green cone deficiency

Blue–Yellow Colorblindness

Less common and affects the ability to tell apart blues from greens and yellows from reds.

  • Tritanomaly / Tritanopia

Complete Colorblindness (Achromatopsia)

A rare condition in which individuals perceive little to no color. It may also cause sensitivity to light and reduced visual clarity.

What Causes Colorblindness?

Most colorblindness is inherited and present from birth. Because the genes controlling color vision are linked to the X chromosome, the condition is more common in men than women.

Color vision deficiency may also be acquired later in life due to:

  • Eye diseases (e.g., glaucoma, macular degeneration)
  • Aging
  • Eye or brain injuries
  • Certain medications or chemical exposure

How Colorblindness Affects Daily Life

While colorblindness does not usually impact overall visual sharpness, it can make tasks such as reading color-coded information, identifying traffic signals, choosing ripe fruits, or selecting matching clothing more challenging. Many individuals use tools like color-identifying apps or specialized lenses to assist with color differentiation.

Is There a Cure?

There is currently no cure for inherited colorblindness. However, assistive technologies, accessibility tools, and adaptive learning strategies can help individuals manage color-dependent tasks. In cases of acquired colorblindness, treating the underlying condition may improve color perception.
